## Introduction

A switched-capacitor (SC) circuit performs its function by transferring charge into and out of capacitors as electronic switches open and close. The switches are typically driven by non-overlapping clock signals to ensure that they are never all closed simultaneously.

## Parallel Resistor SC

The simples circuit that we can build to understand SC is the **Parallel Resistor SC**.

<img src="img/sc_parallel_resistor.png" width=30%>

The theory behind this circuit seems overwhelming at first but it relies on "massaging" a couple of fundamental equations in order to express ohm's law in terms of capacitance and switching frequency.

Ohm's Law: $V = RI$

Since we are dealing with capacitors, let's express voltage and current in terms of charges.

Voltage: $V=\frac{Q}{C}$

Current: $I = \frac{Q}{t}$

Frequency: $f=\frac{1}{t}$

Plugin these equations into Ohm's law and we get the following:

$$R=\frac{V}{I}=\frac{1}{Cf}$$
!!! important
	Notice on how resistance is now a function of **capacitance** and **frequency**. 
	
	This frequency is related with how fast we let current flow through the capacitor, which in turn is **controlled** by opening and closing **S1** and **S2** switches.

!!! tip
	The switches shouldn't overlap, meaning, when S1 is open, S2 should be closed and vice versa.
